## Part 1: before running anything

1. An FFT describes a time-domain signal by showing which are its most important frequency sinusoidal components and how strong they are.
2. - The sampling rate or sampling frequence $f_s$ is the frequence used to scan the signal and is related to the Nyquist frequency through the Sampling Theorem:
   $f_s > 2 \cdot f_n.   $
     It tells us how many samples per second are captured from the signal.
   - The Nyquist frequency is the highest frequency that can be represented faithfully by a DFT.
   - The frequency resolution is the distance between the FFT frequence bins. Given a sampling frequency $f_s$ and a number $N$ of samples, the frequency resolution is defined as $\Delta f = \frac{f_s}{N} = \frac{1}{T},$
   with $T$ the total observation time.

3. The DFT/FFT formula assumes samples at equally-spaced times $t_i = i \cdot \Delta t$.

## Part 3: Sampling and aliasing

Focus on `src/sampling_demo.c`.

1. 
2. The Nyquist frequency $f_N$ for this case is $f_N = 64\mathrm{Hz}$.
3. A $120\mathrm{Hz}$ component cannot be reconstructed faithfully because the Sampling Theorem tells us that the maximum frequency that can be reconstructed with a sampling rate $f_s$ is $f_\mathrm{max} = f_N = \frac{f_s}{2} = 64\mathrm{Hz}$. Any frequence above the Nyquist frequence will appear as an false peak at a lower frequency (aliasing).
4. 
5.
   #### Physical explanation
    Since the sampling is done with discrete points, which are separated in time according to the sampling rate $f_s$, then a high-frequency signal ($f > f_N$) cannot be exactly solved but is mistaken for a false lower-frequency signal, with longer oscillation time $T$, since the points can catch only a longer oscillation.
   #### Mathematical explanation
    The discrete sampling cannot distinguish between the signal produced by a out-of-range frequence $f$ and the one produced from a lower frequence and produce the false signal at $f_{\mathrm{alias}} = \left| f - n f_s \right|$, with $f$ is the true high frequency, $f_s$ the sampling frequency and $n\in\mathbb{Z}$ so that $f_{\mathrm{alias}} < f_N$ is a visible frequence.
  
## Part 4: Frequency resolution

Focus on the `short_record` case.

2. Using $T = N \Delta t$, what is the total record length $T$?
   Since in `src/sampling_demo.c`, the `short_record` case uses $N = 64$ samples and $f_s = 512 \mathrm{Hz}$ and $\Delta t = \frac{1}{f_s}$, it follows $T = 64 \cdot \frac{1}{512} \mathrm{s} = 0.125\mathrm{s}$.
3. The frequency spacing $\Delta f$ of the FFT bins is then $\Delta f = \dfrac{f_s}{N} = \dfrac{512}{64}\mathrm{Hz} = 8\mathrm{Hz}$.
4. The two frequencies of $50\mathrm{Hz}$ and $55\mathrm{Hz}$ are difficult to separate because of the too coarse spacing in the $\Delta f$ grid: $\Delta f = 8\mathrm{Hz} > 55\mathrm{Hz} - 50\mathrm{Hz} = 5\mathrm{Hz}$, so the two peaks overlaps and cannot be distinguished with a $8\mathrm{Hz}$ sensitivity. They will appear as a single broad peak, since they both fall in the same bin.
5. In order to improve the separation I would first change the total acquisition time. In fact, we know that $\Delta f = \dfrac{f_s}{N} = \dfrac{1}{T}$, so the longer is $T$, the finer is the spacing in $\Delta f$, implying that closer and closer peaks can be distinguished properly.

## Part 5: Coupled oscillators

Focus on `src/coupled_oscillators_fft.c`.

1. The equations of motion used in the code are $m \ddot{x}_1 = - (k + k_c) x_1 + k_c x_2$ and $m \ddot{x}_2 = k_c x_1 - (k + k_c) x_2$, where $m$ is the mass of each oscillator, $k$ is the spring constant of the wall springs and $k_c$ is the coupling spring constant.
2. These equations represent two equal masses $m$ which are both connected to a wall by means of a spring with elastic constant $k$ and, additionally, they are connected to each other by a spring with elastic constant $k_c$. 
3. The two normal modes of this system are an in-phase (both masses move in the same direction, coupling spring is unstretched) and an out-of-phase (masses move in opposite directions, coupling spring is compressed/stretched) mode, whose frequencies are $f_{\mathrm{in}} = \frac{1}{2 \pi} \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$ and $f_{\mathrm{out}} = \frac{1}{2 \pi} \sqrt{\frac{k + 2 k_c}{m}}$.
4. The fact that the FFT of $x_1(t)$ shows more than one important frequency depends on the initial condition: in general, it is not a pure normal mode, but a superposition of both normal modes. Each mode oscillates at its own frequency, so $x_1(t)$ beats between them, and the FFT correctly reveals both.
5. Comparing the numerical FFT peaks with the theoretical is a useful verification step because it confirms that the ODE solver algorithm, as well as the sampling of the trajectory and the FFT peak-finding routine, are working correctly and reproduce the actual physics of the system. Small residual differences that may appear in the frequencies are due to numerical errors.
